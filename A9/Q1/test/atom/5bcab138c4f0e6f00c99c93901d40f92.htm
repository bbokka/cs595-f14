thick black rims.

I met a lady who wore thick, black glasses like hula hoops circling her dancing eyes.

Tommy and I have been wanting to tell stories lately. We've been thinking about how to do it. Photos, words, sound bites. I told him, "We need to find people not only with good stories, but good faces, too."

Now, I don't mean pretty...I mean, I do, but not like the magazines print it. I want to print pretty like an egg falling off the counter and cracking in a perfect mess--in the shape of something we can all recognize or relate to.

"Your glasses are adorable," I told the lady.

"Oh, thanks. I get lots of compliments on them," she said as she tipped them off her nose to inspect the rims. "I got them because, you see, I have one fake eye."

She pushed her face an inch forward--permission for me to take a look.

"Oh, I see it now," I said, looking closely. Although I had noticed a difference between the eyes from the moment I met her, now I could see the unique edge of the glass implant and the way it was nestled loosely in its socket, under the thin curtain of her paper-thin lid.

"These glasses make the eye less noticeable."

She put the glasses back on her face. And again, her eyes started swinging their hips--dancing inside those thick, black rims.

caring to know.

I just opened the window of this attic apartment. I can reach out through the opening and put a hand on the sandpaper tiles of the roof. The other day, and I don't remember what triggered it, but Tommy told me, "You love roofs!" And, I know it's a little example, but he knows me so well. Even the quirks! And there are quirks: I don't like socks. I struggle, at times, to know the coordinates of my belongings (fancy way of saying that I lose stuff), and sometimes I have to, as he put it, "try on an idea like a new shirt until it gets comfortable"...He knows all of that. I told him the other day, "You know it all and still love me!" He texted back, "I love you even more now that I know it all!" I think that maybe at least a 1/5th of loving (there are just so many special parts of it) is simply caring to know. Wanting to know what the other person loves, what could just make their day, and also what they're afraid of. 

Loma Linda is warming up. I need to sell everything by the 10th of June so I can move to....well, Washington, for now! When you want to get out for a run, the best first step is to put your shoes on. Moving to Washington is like putting my shoes on. 

Selling everything. Just saying it makes me feel lighter. I moved here with only a car load of things and I think that's how I'll leave. My grandma would kill me if I sold my wooden writing desk, tho. So I'll be keeping that. And I'll keep my road bike, too. But the two work benches, the industrial sewing machine, the set of lawn furniture, the potted plants, the dining room table and four chairs, the three couches, the washing-machine fire pit, the set of gardening tools, the ladder bookshelf, the bookcase, the dresser, the four lamps, the 5 rugs, the five blankets, the coat racks, the blanket racks, and on and on and on...they all need new loving owners.

This weekend my two roommates (both also named Emily) and I tried to summit the highest peak in Southern California. I hope I'm not so blown away by the world simply because I'm young. I hope that's not it. There were so many compelling views, character-filled trees, and stretches of trail that looked liked they'd been edited and prepped for Pinterest, or heaven. 

About a mile from the summit, we hit ice. It was melting. I could see it dripping and running along the trail. But it wasn't melting fast enough. After traversing a few steep and icy portions of the trail, we finally came to a stretch that seemed impassible without crampons and ice axes. But there was a rock fall that wasn't icy, it was just straight steep and full of lose boulders and I said I thought we could make it up and to the next switchback. They weren't convinced. Now, I've never experienced this before, but some fire in me burned hot to reach the summit. Part of it was that I didn't know when I'd ever attempt that climb again. I said, "I'll go check it out." After a two-bloody-ankle inducing scramble up the face, I reached the next portion of trail and I don't know where the energy came from but I just started running, trying to get to figure out how much good trail we had before running into ice again. Long story short, I came back down. We, the three Emilys, decided to surrender the summit. I know that's not that great of a story, but the beauty! It was so good. So compelling.

Sometimes I feel selfish when I'm in the mountains. It's so quiet up high. There isn't phone service. And that disconnectedness makes me feel selfish. But it also makes me feel like myself. Calm and at peace. There's enough quiet to let a clear thought get some attention. At one point on the way down, after we'd eaten snacks and found comfortable ways lay draped over boulders, we fell asleep. My nap was short-lived though and while I was waiting for the others to wake up, I sat on that rock and could feel every shift in wind and sun. Every cloud, every gust and also every bloody ankle and aching muscle. It was the opposite of overstimulated. I sat there waiting to know everything, wanting to take it all in. It was caring to know. It was like selling everything.

the condition of his hands.

Today on my lunch break I was getting gas when a man came walking straight at me holding a sign. His eyes were like stained glass windows, red and fixed somewhere on the back of mine but we weren't really seeing each other. What I mean is, I wasn't sure he could actually see because he looked kind of absent, and then I wasn't truly seeing him either because the closer he got, the more fearful I was of his stare and the size of his body--his body which I could not predict. I started to look down to the sign...and that's when I got caught up by the condition of his hands.

I don't have perfect hands by any means. I get hang nails sometimes when I'm stressed. Also, I can count on just one of my imperfect hands the number of manicures I've had in my life. But this man's fingers could have brought me to tears had I not been so stressed and afraid. It was the cracks, the peeling, the stiffness, the dirt. It was the way I could see they hadn't been washed or held in too long. It was so potentially heartbreaking. In fact, I could feel my heart starting to break…and that's when I left. It's not right, but it's honest.

I'm not saying this is how it should be. In fact, I'm looking for a solution to simply walking away. I don't read the news anymore because last time I did I was shaken up for a week by the man that tied a rope around his neck, tied it to his steering wheel, and then jumped off a bridge down the street with his car in drive--just to make sure he could be done with this world.

I know that helping starts in your own house, your own workplace. And I know it's a leap--love is a leap--to risk one's heart. But how, as Bob Goff advises, do you open your heart while guarding it? Is that how it's done? Is that a good theory? I feel like I've just been given a new heart from God lately, a new spirit, one's that's less anxious and more resilient. And deep down I know I'm feeling selfish with it. Feeling protective.

People say that God will refill you as you give. But I'm a skeptic of that kind of simplicity. I think its more difficult than that. But I do believe in a blessing I don't understand, as Sara Groves says. And I do believe that the good things of God--his blessings--are not things, they are not in the condition of our hands or the prosperity found in our wallets. His blessings are things like the promise of his face in the future, a transforming kind of grace for the present, and a peace that surpasses our understanding.

One time my friend Alexandra told me, Emily, I pray for you all the time, but what specifically can I pray for you about? And if anyone had room on the list of things they say to God, I would be so grateful for prayer in this area. I know there are much more urgent things happening to be prayed about, but if I could find the way forward, maybe I could start watching the news again and doing something about it.

In the meantime, I feel like the prayer below is one I should be praying:

Disturb us, Lord, when
We are too well pleased with ourselves,
When our dreams have come true
Because we have dreamed too little,
When we arrived safely
Because we sailed too close to the shore.

Disturb us, Lord, when
With the abundance of things we possess
We have lost our thirst
For the waters of life;
Having fallen in love with life,
We have ceased to dream of eternity
And in our efforts to build a new earth,
We have allowed our vision
Of the new Heaven to dim.

Disturb us, Lord, to dare more boldly,
To venture on wider seas
Where storms will show your mastery;
Where losing sight of land,
We shall find the stars.

We ask You to push back
The horizons of our hopes;
And to push into the future
In strength, courage, hope, and love.

On another note, the traffic is so buzz-y here in Southern California that sometimes when I'm outside during the middle of the day, the buzz makes me damn straight crazy! It could drive me to drive until I could be alone or hear a quiet minute. Since that's hard to do and also to keep your job, I just sit inside my car while the gas is pumping to keep from being subject to the buzz.

sweaty hug for Bob Goff.

I feel like theres a lot to write about these days but I don't write any of it. I feel very private. (Which is weird cause I'm not that private of a person.) Why? I don't really know. Part of it, I guess, is that most of my day is taken up by work, and although a lot of stuff happens at work, I don't feel quite right about writing about it. And then there's the way that being out of school makes me feel like somehow, magically, all of the ups and downs of life should have come to a quiet glassy stillness, and that I should have grown out of things like questions and bad days and irrational thoughts by now. Obviously that's not the case for any of us. And while I do think there is a kind of peace and settledness that comes with age (one of my biggest fears is that I'll someday discover that what I hoped would be peace is really only apathy), I don't think a bad day is a crime.

I think part of it, too, is that I'm still sorting out what it means to be professional. I wrote a bit about the topic in writing school: about the time I showed up to play harp and the lady in charge of the venue had blanketed the corner where I was to play in white pillow stuffing.  She said she hoped it would be like I was playing "in the heavens" and within an hour, to make a long story short, I was wearing angel wings and a white feather boa. I felt like a circus act that night, not necessarily what I'd been taught was professional by my strict and staunch harp teacher. And yet there have been few performances where I've ever connected to a group of people quite like that. I didn't feel very "professional" and yet in some ways, the gig went off better and was more of a success than what I'd considered the more "professional" performances. So it kind of changed how I saw that word.

Another reason maybe I've been halted in writing is the whole transitioning thing. I think we as humans continually seek to define ourselves and so at each juncture (new school, new job, new phase, new relationship) we're trying to regain a sense of "self" in that setting. I heard something in a Bob Goff talk this weekend that was really great. He said, "Everybody, just be yourself," and then gave the advice that the best way to get back to being yourself was to love others. It was simple, but if you think about it….the reason we become someone other than ourselves is that we begin to pursue another image, another future self that we envision or that others have envisioned for us. We begin to reinvent ourselves to get where we need to go and we end up not being ourselves at all. Bob Goff suggested that if we're loving others, our actions will be the most authentic because they're not motivated with any inward-oriented compass. The most authentic "you" comes out when you're distracted enough by the needs of others to let your actions spring forth naturally. I liked that. It resonated.

I actually got to meet Bob Goff (P.S. He is the author of the book Love Does). I attended his talk on Monday mid-day but had to run all the way across campus to get a photo of the technical exhibit award ceremony for work. And I do mean that I RAN. I full-out sprinted over there, got the shot, and then sprinted back because I didn't want to miss the rest of the talk. But by the time I got there, sweating and breathing hard, the talk was over and the crowd was making its way out the exits like a milkshake out the end of a thin straw. I was standing there by the side entrance, squinting my eyes and waiting for them to adjust, when all of a sudden, this guy says, "Hi! I'm Bob! What's your name?" My eyes were adjusted enough to see two long arms stretched out to me. (Bob doesn't believe in handshakes--just hugs, "but not the creepy kind.") Soon my sweaty body was wrapped up in a hug from Bob Goff and he said, "Nice to see ya!" Which was another of the main points in his talk: that we don't need to just meet people, we need to really see them. And not just see them, but see them for who they are becoming. Knowing his perspective on "seeing"  made me feel better about what it was he was actually seeing and about the sweaty-frazzled-redhead thing. It felt freeing to know that he actually probably believed I was much, much more than what he saw.

leaning in.

Yesterday, Roxy (78) and I spent all day together at the APC registration booth. At one point, we were talking about husbands. Well, hers. ;)

She's had one for 44 years and says that he's eaten raspberries for breakfast almost every Saturday morning since they got together. "He seems to think he'll die if he doesn't have them," she said affectionately. "It's kind of nice," she said, "I never wonder what he'll want to eat on that morning."

Roxy leans in to people...if that makes sense. For example, when we laughed, her whole body tilted toward me. And I always found that it prompted me to lean in too. And so there we were like a little a-frame whenever something was funny or sweet.

running together.

Tonight I took pictures of this family for an Alumni Journal piece I'm working on. Marathon-running physicians. This particular physician is a pediatrician and she and her husband are both marathon runners. When the mom gets back from her long runs, she comes in the door and asks the boys, "Who wants to go for a mile with me." She says she only takes them one at a time because they are too competitive to go together. Oh and they are FAST. I was scrambling up the hill in my boat shoes after them, trying to get ahead to get the shot that I needed. (I had boat shoes full of dirt by the end.) 

Seeing their family tonight, I was reminded of something I hope for in life. I want to grow and build a family that does things together. Sleepovers, tea parties, bike rides, art projects, making meals, super-secret-stealth-delivery-of-good-things-on-people's-doorsteps. And also, I want us to learn how to talk to each other. I was talking to Ashley about that the other night. About family conferences like Nilmini's family had. I like that so much.

Another thing: I've been watching this little movie whenever I need to be inspired to feel. This precious boy with a heart of wax. I love him. And I hope I can find all the ways to open my kids hearts to love: keep them safe enough to be trusting, loved enough to be generous, and stunned enough times into a state of wonder by the beauty in the world that wonder becomes a thing they know and a way they see.


I also hope that I'll be good at pointing out the way God moves and the way he sweeps up the pieces after the lamp crashes and the way he coordinates some of our most joyful, trampoline-jumping-baby-chick-hatching-moments. I know God is always moving and working...(I know that now)…and I think sometimes we all grow a whole lot if someone in our life has the habit of seeing Love in every situation and also the courage to talk about it. I hope I can develop that habit.

I'm tuckered. These work days are weird and good at the same time. I don't always feel efficient (I had hoped to do laundry tonight) but I feel like I'm growing and that's important, that's a thing I need to learn to accept.
