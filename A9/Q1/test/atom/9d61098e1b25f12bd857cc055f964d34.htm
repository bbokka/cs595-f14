Monday, May 24

I Dare You To Find Something More American Than This "Sandwich".

I'm sure you all have seen the commercials for KFC's new Double Down sandwich.
Yeah, that thing. "So meaty, there's no room for a bun."
Well, I recently tried one, largely out of curiosity.
Nutritional Value: Don't really care that much. (I'm a guy, what do you expect?) According to the KFC website, the one with crispy chicken has 540 calories, in case you're curious.
Taste: Eh. Was alright. The question is largely moot anyway - KFC is still going to make money off of this thing regardless.

No, in my opinion, the main problem with the Double Down is in the very concept of the thing - ratios. Those who love sandwiches - such as myself - should know exactly what I mean.

Have you ever had a sandwich that had too much mayo or not enough ham or something like that? RATIOS. While individual tastes differ, I think we can all agree that nothing should be too overpowering in a sandwich - the tastes should be of relatively equal strength, so that they can blend together. I know that the commercial advertises that it has two pieces of bacon and two slices of cheese, but on the one I was served, there was only one of each, and the taste of chicken really overpowered the bacon, cheese...and whatever that sauce thing was.

Another thing, regarding the whole "no bread" deal. In a traditional sandwich, the bread sort of acts like a blank canvas, upon which can be painted tasty, delicious art. With the Double Down, I feel like it's just...paint. No canvas, so to speak. This isn't necessarily a bad thing, but the Double Down feels like it should be eaten with a knife and fork.

If you haven't had a Double Down, you're not missing much. You can get pretty much the same thing - on bread, no less - at your local deli. It'll probably taste better, too, and you get to support local businesses while you're at it.

Jim actually blogging? An unbelievable phenomenon, I know.

I'm always incredibly surprised and flattered - largely the former - whenever people tell me in real life that they (consistently, no less) read my blog.

Amateur Conductor, Professional Nostalgiophile

Today I bought a conductor's baton, somewhat on a whim. It was five bucks from the local Mom & Pop music shop. It's basically a stick with a painted "handle" (a rather nice stick, at that).
I kinda consider conducting to be my dream Plan B career, if the whole directing thing doesn't work out (no, I would not be surprised to find myself as a bohemian ten years from now, why do you ask?). At the very least, when I pace around conducting whatever song happens to be in my head, I no longer have to do it with just my hands or, say, a pen.

I tend to name my instruments. My bass clarinet is Bessie, my tenor sax is Lucy, and my accordion is 'Cordeen. (Oddly enough, my soprano clarinet, despite being the instrument I've had the longest, doesn't have a name. I guess we've been together so long we've grown past the need for names. Or something.) I felt it would only be appropriate to name my baton. I first tried "Charlotte", after the girl from summer camp who recently passed away. The name immediately stuck.

Tuesday, December 8

Rest In Peace, Charlotte

I just found out that a girl I went to summer camp with several years ago recently committed suicide. I didn't know her very well, and thus do not know her reasons for taking her own life, but I'm struck nonetheless.

The camp - Johns Hopkins University Center for Talented Youth (CTY for short) - was like a second home to me when I was in high school. Even though we began as strangers, coming from different places and different backgrounds, we were able to forge iron-clad friendships. We looked forward to the the three-week sessions all year; they came and went all too quickly.

As the swarm of Facebook statuses dedicated to Charlotte grew, conversations started with several people from CTY I haven't seen or spoken to in years. I'm happy to know that we CTYers are always there for one another. I soon realized, though, how incredibly few CTYers I've kept in regular touch with. While I know and fully realize that children grow up and friends grow apart, I'm still depressed that it took something like this to get all of us to start talking again.
