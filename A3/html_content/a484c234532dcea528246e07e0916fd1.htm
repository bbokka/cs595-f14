Monday, 25 June 2012

The calm after the storm

The heavens opened last week and my garden turned into a river. Don't get me wrong, we weren't flooded like the poor people of Hebden Bridge or Todmorden, but water flowed across the lawn nevertheless. 

I have a question for anyone who is likely to read this (hint of hopelessness in my voice). I planted all these courgettes at the same time, the soil in both levels of the plot is the same, all were started off in pots indoors, the only difference is that the back ones were planted out a week later than the front. Taking into consideration that they have been out in the garden for weeks, why haven't they caught up. Perhaps they are doing it out of spite!

I found out last night that some silly soul has poured a whole load of tarmac at some time in the dim past onto part of the garden where I wanted to plant some fruit bushes. I felt rather like an archaeologist yesterday evening carefully scraping with a trowel round a large flat surface under the soil. Perhaps it's a beautiful buried Belfast sink that I can plant alpines in, perhaps it is the remains of some really nice garden paving - no, it's a huge chunk of tarmac which will have to be removed by pick axe!

On the up side the plot is starting to yield some beautiful bounty, we have had a weekend of eating gooseberries.

When the sun shines I have to admit the garden is starting to look rather pretty.

Monday, 30 April 2012

Our love/hate relationship with weeds

For several years now I have been battling with an interloper in my garden, It is definitely here uninvited, spreads itself round with unseemly abandon but causes no damage other than placing the colour orange where I feel it has no right to be. I have resented my profligate interloper for a long time, I have spent many (well, not that many) back breaking hours on my knees digging it out of the soil, but it is clearly still there.

I remember reading somewhere the statement that 'weeds' are simply flowers in the wrong place. Try telling that to someone with a garden full of Japanese Knotweed. Something happened this weekend that made me scratch my head and think about just that, maybe it isn't that weeds are flowers in the wrong place, but more that they are flowers with the wrong names. After all Japanese Knotweed sounds strangulating and unpleasant doesn't it? In which case the opposite could possibly be true, a weed/flower causing a nuisance, if provided with the correct name, seems less of a problem.

Back to this weekend's event. The Observer in Sunday arrived with a small guide to Britain's wild flowers and leafing casually through, what should take pride of place on page 20 but my Public Enemy Number One - Pilosella aurantiaca.

Would you like to meet the profligate little devil, and before you do, would you like to know its common name? Fox and Cubs! Makes you feel all warm and fuzzy doesn't it? Me too. I shall still be weeding the little fellas out of my flower border because they do seem to breed like rabbits (by runners by the way, not actual plant sex), but I may just let them flower occasionally just so that I can say that I have a Fox and Cubs in the garden.

Monday, 10 October 2011

Glutney Chutney

Every year the time comes when you stand in the garden, stare at the allotment, scratch your head and think "what the **** am I going to do with all those marrows!" You know that you shouldn't have let them grow that big, you know that you shouldn't have gone away on holiday and left a non-courgette eating neighbour look after the plot and you know you should have cut your losses and given them all away. But there they are, just sitting there and staring back up at you saying "we're enormous and green and what the **** are you going to do with us?"



This is almost direct theft from Hugh F-W's 'The River Cottage Cookbook', but I've pinched it from 'Preserves' by Pam Corbin, with a hint to Nigel Slater's Mixed Tomato Chutney. I hope none of them will sue me, but I have made the small addition of cardamom to the mix for no better reason than that I adore it!

Ingredients
(this makes just a couple of jars of chutney)

450g marrows (peeled and diced)
450g green tomatoes
200g cooking apples (I use windfalls from the garden) peeled, cored and diced
350g onions peeled and diced
90g sultanas
250g light soft brown sugar
300ml cider vinegar
2tsp yellow mustard seeds
12 cardamom pods (husks removed but don't crush seeds)
1 tsp salt
1 medium sized red chili
a good grind of black pepper

Put all the ingredients, except the tomatoes in a heavy bottomed pan (I have just shelled out for a proper preserving 'jam pan' this year, but in previous year I have managed perfectly well in a big saucepan).
Bring everything to the boil and leave to simmer for an hour, stirring it occasionally to prevent it from sticking.
After about 25 minutes add the tomatoes and finish the cooking time.
Spoon the delicious sticky stuff into sterilized jars and seal.
This tastes better if you leave it for a while before you eat it (that's my advice, however I usually get stuck into it straight away).

Can I just add that this is a perfectly brilliant accompaniment for plain cheese on toast!

Thursday, 16 June 2011

My mini allotment is growing up

We have a had a lot or rain and I MEAN a lot, but the little sunshine we
have had seems to be doing the trick, my vegetables are growing. I have never claimed to know what I am doing in the garden department and all that I have planted this year are really experiments, but it is looking good.



In the garden this year:
Courgettes (both yellow and green)
Chard (both plain and 'bright lights')
Dwarf beans
Mangetouts
Radish
Sweet spherical carrots
Pac choi
Celeriac
Kale
Sweetcorn
Strawberries
Rhubarb
Redcurrants
Gooseberries

It's amazing what you can shoe horn into a tiny space. Not all of it will be successful I know, but I'm enjoying that thrill of checking the plot out every morning to see how it's going. I have to add that the last four items on the list are not in the raised bed, but next to it on the railway banking.


This Kale is dedicated to my dad (who built the raised bed) since he has informed me that he will be eating most of it!



I know that this blog doesn't have a scratch and sniff facility, but I can guarantee that these strawberries and radishes were excellent!

My only problem with having my own mini allotment is that I really should be keeping some sort of diary as a record of what was planted on which date, germination times and harvesting comments. If I put them in my blog I would probably bore people to death, if in fact anyone out there reads this (comments please!). In the meantime I will muddle on in disorganized chaos as per the rest of my life. I have kept three children alive and healthy, so I'm sure my garden will cope.

Wednesday, 18 May 2011

An allotment is born


When will this rain ever end, I half expect Noah to wave at me from his ark as he drifts down the road. Towards the end of the beautiful spring weather I half remember enjoying Dad and I took on a building project, to create a mini allotment in my garden.

In case I haven’t explained this before, our house is
situated next to a local rail branch line and despite the novelty of having our
very own train set running through the garden , it also means that half of our
garden slopes at an alarming angle.

With timber from a local merchant my dad built a raised bed
in two sections which, when filled with soil, should give plenty of growing
room, the added advantage being that drainage should not be a problem.

Once the sides went on I couldn’t wait to get it filled and planted
and then the heavens opened for days on end, leaving me frustrated and with
trays full of vegetable seedlings ready to go in the soil.

The finished bed,
however looks wonderful – thanks Dad!

Sunday, 17 April 2011

Great bits of Liverpool




We've been visiting Liverpool for years, trailing round the shops and declaring every time that we wished we'd gone to Manchester instead (please don't have a go at me ... I have university allegiances with Manchester and can't help it!).



Today has been quite a revelation, getting slightly off the beaten track and out of the deadly boring Liverpool One, the photo opportunities have been glorious. The side streets remind me of Spitalfield and parts of the east end of London. One hopes that then powers that be will not rush to gentrify them, an action which will destroy their filthy charm. Mind you, under the present government, there's not much chance of that (now, now, no politics!). Liverpool has some of the most beautiful architecture in the north of England if you look behind the grime.


One place in particular took me by surprise, the beautiful bombed out church of St. Luke on the hill above the city centre. Once a haunt of the marginalized and dispossessed, a neglected memorial to the victims of the bombing of Liverpool during WW2 and a rapidly disintegrating heap, it has now become a strange non religious spiritual magnet for those who drift in. Entry is only gained after signing a disclaimer rendering the owners blameless if you die a horrible death as the building collapses on you.


The interior, which is really an exterior since there is no roof, has the ability to give you the shivers. On the day we visited Bach was playing loudly to the skies and filling every stone and alcove, flowers grew along the nave and photographs of the homeless of the city adorned the walls. I had to resist the urge to lie on the grass among the flowers, soak up the music and forget the outside world.
