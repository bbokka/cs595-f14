[19]Last post by JupaFX - 5.9.13

   Hello to all English speaking readers. As is often said, there is a
   right time for everything. The time for blogging - for me - has passed.
   And now I'll explain why. It will be a long story, so make yourself
   comfortable.
   In October 2010, I decided to leave a steady job for an unstable job. I
   decided to switch from having a steady paycheck at the end of the
   month, to trading for a living - thinking I would have been able to
   handle it. I found out right away - within a few weeks - that watching
   the markets and making decisions on paper, are not the same thing as
   trading for a living. So I initially failed. I did not loose a lot of
   money - I stopped as soon as I realized there was something wrong.
   Until then I thought I knew enough about the financial markets, and
   forex in particular. I had worked for a broker , I knew the market
   movers , I was attentive to geo-political news, I knew the theory of
   trading - that is, what the books tell you. I had read many books. Too
   many. I was absolutely full of knowledge that I could not turn into
   winning trades.
   So I had to let go. I looked for another job, and I had to accept the
   feeling of humility that every private trader has ( in my opinion). I
   just was not initially willing to admit that I could not do it. Now, I
   know why: I diddn't have the correct mindset and knowledge to make it.
   I did not understand maket dynamics. I did not have the ability to read
   a chart without any (useless) indicators on it. Well...I did not really
   have the right skills to do it. But I did have one thing: the courage
   to follow my ideas. The courage to try again.
   I actually had something else: a girlfriend just as courageous and
   altruistic. When, in March 2012, we were both unemployed, she sat next
   to me and together we started from scratch. The presentation "what I've
   learned in 1 year of trading" really should be called "What we learned
   in 1 year of trading" because my girlfriend was always by my side and I
   was really surprised. I had the ability to do great research, and the
   intuition to understand if i had hit a brick wall... but she had the
   makings of a trader. She understood whether we were heading in the
   right direction or not. She has the mental strength to suffer 1000
   losses in a row and still have the confidence to try again .
   Together, we have accomplished a small miracle. We did not have the
   savings to do what we had to do. We are still not out of danger, but at
   least now we have the right skills to succeed.
   So dear readers, the blog is no longer needed. Since August, we have
   been operating on FX (where we started), Stocks and Bonds. Because if
   you understand how one financial market works, you can also understand
   other markets. They are all governed by the same reasoning. They are
   all places where individuals meet and exchange views and it is for this
   reason that speculators from the early 1900s can still teach us how
   it's done: the psychology of the market has never changed.
   Now I leave you with the presentation, which I hope you find amusing.
   The blog will remain active but I will not be writing any more. I will,
   however, always be available on twitter @JupaFX and of course via
   email: jupafx@gmail.com
   Enjoy the presentation and good luck!

[31]Ultimo Post di JupaFX - 5.9.13

   Buongiorno a tutti i lettori italiani. Come spesso si dice, c'è un
   momento per ogni cosa ed una cosa per ogni momento. Il momento del
   blog, per me, è passato. Ed ora vi spiego perchè. Sarà una lunga
   storia, quindi mettetevi comodi.
   Nell'ottobre del 2010, ho deciso di lasciare un lavoro stabile per un
   lavoro instabile. Ho deciso di passare dalla busta paga di fine mese al
   trading - pensando di saperci fare. Ho scoperto subito - nel giro di
   poche settimane - che osservare i mercati e prendere decisioni su
   carta, non sono la medesima cosa del fare trading per lavoro. Quindi ho
   inizialmente fallito. Non ho perso molti soldi - mi sono fermato appena
   ho capito che c'era qualcosa che non andava.
   Fino ad allora pensavo di saperne abbastanza sui mercati finanziari, e
   sul forex in particolare. Avevo lavorato per un broker, conoscevo i
   market mover, ero attento alle notizie geo-politiche, sapevo bene la
   teoria del trading - cioè quello che i libri ti dicono. Avevo letto
   tantissimi libri. Troppi. Ero assolutamente colmo di nozioni che però
   non riuscivo a trasformare in operazioni vincenti.
   Quindi ho dovuto mollare la presa. Ho cercato un altro lavoro, ed ho
   dovuto accogliere il sentimento di umiltà che ogni trader privato
   possiede (secondo me). Semplicemente non ero inizialmente disposto ad
   ammettere che non potevo farcela. Ora, so anche perchè: non mi
   conoscevo. Non conoscevo la dinamica di mercato. Non avevo la capacità
   di leggere un grafico senza sovrapporre indicatori (inutili).
   Insomma...non avevo proprio le competenze giuste per farcela. Ma una
   cosa ce l'avevo: il coraggio di seguire le mie idee. Il coraggio di
   tentare ancora, dopo aver fallito.
   In realtà avevo anche un'altra cosa: una fidanzata altrettanto
   coraggiosa e tanto altruista. Quando, nel marzo del 2012, siamo rimasti
   entrambi disoccupati, lei si è seduta accanto a me ed insieme siamo
   partiti da zero. La presentazione "quello che ho imparato in 1 anno di
   trading" in realtà dovrebbe chiamarsi "quello che ABBIAMO imparato in 1
   anno di trading" perchè la mia fidanzata è stata sempre al mio fianco e
   mi ha davvero sorpreso. Io avevo la capacità di fare grandi ricerche,
   di intuire la strada giusta...e lei aveva la stoffa del trader. Lei
   aveva il fiuto per capire se stavamo imboccando una direzione giusta o
   una direzione sbagliata. Lei aveva la forza mentale di subìre 1000
   perdite di fila e non abbattersi.
   Insieme, abbiamo compiuto un piccolo miracolo. Non avevamo
   assolutamente i risparmi per fare quello che dovevamo fare. Tutt'ora
   non siamo fuori pericolo, ma almeno ora abbiamo le competenze giuste
   per farcela.
   Quindi cari lettori, il blog non serve più. Da agosto, siamo operativi
   su FX (da dove siamo partiti), Azioni e obbligazioni. Perchè se capisci
   un mercato finanziario, puoi capire anche gli altri mercati. Sono tutti
   governati dagli stessi ragionamenti. Sono tutti luoghi di incontro tra
   individui ed è per questo che gli speculatori di 100 anni fa possono
   far scuola anche oggi: la psicologia del mercato non è mai cambiato.
   Ora vi lascio alla presentazione, che spero sia divertente. Il blog
   rimarrà attivo ma non pubblicherò più. Sarò invece sempre reperibile su
   twitter @JupaFX e ovviamente tramite email: jupafx@gmail.com
   Buona lettura e buona fortuna!

[43]Thoughts on Trading & Life - 3.9.13

   "A healthy attitude is contagious but don't wait to catch it from
   others. Be a carrier!"
   - Barrie Sheply
   A book I read during the holidays was the Michael Phelps biography by
   Alan Abrahamson. With eight gold medals at the Beijing Olympic Games,
   Phelps became the most successful Olympian in any sport (ever). At
   fifteen years of age, Phelps got his feet wet by making the Sydney
   Olympic Games Team for the USA and narrowly missed a medal. Four years
   later in 2004, Phelps went after Mark Spitz record of seven gold medals
   and while he came up a bit short he did show signs of his future
   greatness.
   Then, with the only coach he has ever had, Phelps beat the odds by
   winning his eight gold medals at the Olympics. The book shows how
   important Phelps's attitude was in his over all success. I loved his
   mantra of W-I-N. What's Important Now (WIN). Phelps's philosophy is
   WHAT can I do right now to help improve my situation? A great example
   of his philosophy in action occurred during the Beijing Olympic when
   his goggles filled up with water. During the actual Olympic final, his
   swim goggles filled up with so much water that he couldn't see
   anything. To prepare for anything, Phelps actually practiced swimming
   with goggles filled (just in case he might ever encounter this
   situation in an event). Michael Phelps didn't panic when it occurred at
   the Olympics and his planning helped him win. Instead of panicking,
   Phelps had the mental acuity to count his swim strokes as he blindly
   swam down the pool. Michael knew it should take him 19 strokes to get
   to the end of the pool, and even though he couldn't see anything, his
   thousands of hours of practices taught him to trust his body and his
   instincts. The former grade school kid who was put on medication for
   his attention deficit showed that he could focus on his most important
   swim of his career.
