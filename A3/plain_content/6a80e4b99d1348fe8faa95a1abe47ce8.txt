   Satan ([14]Hebrew: ◊©÷∏÷º◊Ç◊ò÷∏◊ü satan, meaning "adversary"^[15][1]) is a
   term, as well as the name of a figure appearing in the texts of the
   [16]Abrahamic religions^[17][2]^[18][3] who brings [19]evil and
   temptation, and is known as the deceiver that leads humanity astray.
   Some religious groups teach that he originated as an [20]angel who fell
   out of favor with God, seducing humanity into the ways of [21]sin, and
   who now rules over the fallen world. In the [22]Hebrew Bible and the
   [23]New Testament, Satan is primarily an accuser and adversary, a
   decidedly malevolent entity (a.k.a. the [24]devil) who possesses
   demonic qualities.

   In [25]Theistic Satanism, Satan is considered a positive force and
   deity who is either worshipped or revered. In [26]LaVeyan Satanism,
   Satan is regarded as holding virtuous characteristics.^[27][4]^[28][5]

Book of Job

   The examination of Job, Satan pours on the plagues of [78]Job, by
   [79]William Blake

   At the beginning of the book, [80]Job is a good person "who revered God
   and turned away from evil" (Job 1:1), and has therefore been rewarded
   by God. When the angels present themselves to God, Satan comes as well.
   God informs Satan about Job's blameless, morally upright character.
   Between Job 1:9‚Äì10 and 2:4‚Äì5, Satan points out that God has given Job
   everything that a man could want, so of course Job would be loyal to
   God; if all Job has been given, even his health, were to be taken away
   from him, however, his faith would collapse. God therefore gives Satan
   permission to test Job.^[81][13] In the end Job remains faithful and
   righteous, with by implication Satan shamed in his defeat.^[82][14]

Second Temple period

Septuagint

   In the [83]Septuagint the Hebrew ha-Satan in Job and [84]Zechariah is
   translated by the [85]Greek word diabolos (slanderer), the same word in
   the [86]Greek New Testament from which the English word [87]devil is
   derived. Where satan is used of human enemies in the [88]Hebrew Bible,
   such as [89]Hadad the Edomite and [90]Rezon the Syrian, the word is
   left untranslated but transliterated in the Greek as satan, a
   [91]neologism in Greek.^[92][15] In Zechariah 3 this changes the vision
   of the conflict over [93]Joshua the High Priest in the Septuagint into
   a conflict between "[94]Jesus and the devil," identical with the Greek
   text of [95]Matthew.

Dead Sea scrolls and Pseudepigrapha

   In Enochic Judaism, the concept of Satan being an opponent of God and a
   chief evil figure in among demons, seems to have taken root in Jewish
   [96]pseudepigrapha during the [97]Second Temple period,^[98][16]
   particularly in the apocalypses.^[99][17] The [100]Book of Enoch
   contains references to [101]Satariel, thought also to be [102]Sataniel
   and [103]Satan'el (etymology dating back to [104]Babylonian origins).
   The similar spellings mirror that of his angelic brethren [105]Michael,
   [106]Raphael, [107]Uriel and [108]Gabriel, previous to the [109]fall
   from Heaven.

   The [110]Second Book of Enoch, also called the Slavonic Book of Enoch,
   contains references to a [111]Watcher (Grigori) called
   Satanael.^[112][18] It is a [113]pseudepigraphic text of an uncertain
   date and unknown authorship. The text describes Satanael as being the
   prince of the Grigori who was cast out of heaven^[114][19] and an evil
   spirit who knew the difference between what was "righteous" and
   "sinful".^[115][20] A similar story is found in the book of [116]1
   Enoch; however, in that book, the leader of the Grigori is called
   [117]Semj√¢z√¢.

   In the [118]Book of Wisdom, the devil is represented as the being who
   brought death into the world.^[119][21]

   [120]Mastema, in the [121]Book of Jubilees, induces God to test
   [122]Abraham through the sacrifice of [123]Isaac. He is identical to
   Satan in both name and nature.^[124][22]

Rabbinical Judaism

   In [125]Judaism, Satan is a term used since its earliest biblical
   contexts, to refer to a human opponent.^[126][23] Occasionally, the
   term has been used to suggest evil influence opposing human beings as
   in the [127]Jewish exegesis of the [128]Yetzer hara ("evil inclination"
   Genesis 6:5). [129]Micaiah's "lying spirit" in 1 Kings 22:22 is
   sometimes related. Thus, Satan is personified as a character in three
   different places of the [130]Tenakh, serving as an accuser (Zechariah
   3:1-2), a seducer (1 Chronicles 21:1), or as a heavenly persecutor who
   is "among the sons of God" (Job 2:1). In any case, Satan is always
   subordinate to the power of God, having a role in the divine plan.
   Satan is rarely mentioned in [131]Tannaitic literature, but is found in
   Babylonian [132]aggadah.^[133][17]

   In [134]Medieval Judaism, the Rabbis rejected these Enochic literary
   works into the [135]Biblical canon, making every attempt to root them
   out.^[136][16] Traditionalists and philosophers in medieval Judaism,
   adhered to [137]rational theology, rejecting any belief in rebel or
   fallen angels, and viewing [138]evil as abstract.^[139][24] The
   [140]Yetzer hara ("evil inclination" Genesis 6:5) is a more common
   motif for evil in rabbinical texts. Rabbinical scholarship on the
   [141]Book of Job generally follows the Talmud and Maimonides as
   identifying the "Adversary" in the prologue of Job as a
   metaphor.^[142][25]

   In [143]Hasidic Judaism, the [144]Kabbalah presents Satan as an agent
   of God whose function is to tempt one into [145]sin, then turn around
   and accuse the sinner on high.^[[146]vague] The [147]Chasidic Jews of
   the 18th century associated ha-Satan with Baal Davar.^[148][26]

Dualism and Zoroastrianism

   See also: [149]Angra Mainyu

   Some scholars see contact with religious [150]dualism in [151]Babylon,
   and early [152]Zoroastrianism in particular, as having influenced
   Second Temple period Judaism, and consequently early
   Christianity.^[153][27]^[154][28] Subsequent development of Satan as a
   "deceiver" has parallels with the evil spirit in [155]Zoroastrianism,
   known as [156]the Lie, who directs forces of darkness.^[157][29]

       that he tempted humankind for "an unspecified sinful reason" and
       was cursed as a result.^[179][33] However, after Justin, the idea

       somewhat later by Tertullian.^[180][34]

       theology to refer to Satan, as a result of identifying the fallen
       "son of the dawn" of [182]Isaiah 14:12 with the "accuser" of other
       passages in the Old Testament.

   In traditional Christian understanding of the holy Hebrew scriptures,
   the [194]Torah, Satan is a synonym for the [195]Devil. For most
   Christians, he is believed to be an [196]angel who rebelled against
   God‚Äîand also the one who spoke through the [197]serpent and seduced Eve
   into disobeying God's command. His ultimate goal is to lead people away
   from the love of God‚Äîto lead them to fallacies which God opposes. Satan
   is also identified as the accuser of [198]Job, the tempter in the
   [199]Gospels, the secret power of lawlessness in [200]2 Thessalonians
   2:7, and the dragon in the Book of Revelation. Before his insurrection,
   Satan was among the highest of all angels and the "[201]brightest in
   the sky". His pride is considered a reason why he would not bow to God
   as all other angels did, but sought to rule heaven himself. The
   popularly held beliefs that Satan was once an angel who becomes
   prideful and eventually rebels against God, however, are not portrayed
   explicitly in the Bible and are mostly based on inference (e.g.,
   [202]Ezekiel 28 and [203]Isaiah 14:12‚Äì17). In mainstream Christianity
   he is called "the ruler of the demons" ([204]Matthew 12:24), "the ruler
   of the world" and "the god of this world" (2 Cor. 4:4). The Book of
   Revelation describes how Satan will be cast out of Heaven, down to the
   earth, having "great anger" and waging war against "those who obey
   God's commandments and hold to the testimony of [205]Jesus".
   Ultimately, Satan is thrown into the "[206]lake of fire", not as ruler,
   but as one among many, being tormented day and night forever and
   ever.^[207][37]

   The early Christian church encountered opposition from pagans such as
   [208]Celsus who claimed "it is blasphemy...to say that the greatest
   God...has an adversary who constrains his capacity to do good" and says
   that Christians "impiously divide the kingdom of God, creating a
   rebellion in it, as if there were opposing factions within the divine,
   including one that is hostile to God".^[209][38]

   In other Christian beliefs (e.g. the beliefs of the
   [210]Christadelphians) the word "satan" in the Bible is not regarded as
   referring to a supernatural, personal being but to any "adversary" and
   figuratively refers to human sin and temptation.^[211][39]

Yazidism

   An alternate name for the main deity in the tentatively
   [235]Indo-European pantheon of the [236]Yazidi, [237]Malek Taus, is
   Shaitan.^[238][45] Rather than Satanic, however, Yazidism is better
   understood as a remnant of a pre-Islamic [239]Middle Eastern
   [240]Indo-European religion, and/or a [241]ghulat [242]Sufi movement
   founded by [243]Shaykh Adi. The connection with Satan, originally made
   by Muslim outsiders, attracted the interest of 19th century
   [244]European travelers and esoteric writers.

Satanism

   Main article: [251]Satanism

   Satanic groups have various opinions about Satan, ranging from the
   conviction that he exists and ought to be worshipped ([252]theistic
   Satanism), to [253]Anton Szandor LaVey's symbolic interpretation, which
   emphasizes individual will and pleasure-seeking. In [254]Theistic
   Satanism, Satan is considered a positive force and deity who is either
   worshipped or revered.^[255][49] In [256]LaVeyan Satanism, Satan is
   regarded as holding virtuous characteristics.^[257][50]

   Among non-Satanists, much modern Satanic folklore does not originate
   with the beliefs or practices of [258]theistic or [259]LeVeyan
   Satanists, but a mixture of medieval Christian folk beliefs, political
   or sociological conspiracy theories, and contemporary urban
   legends.^[260][51]^[261][52]^[262][53]^[263][54] An example is the
   [264]Satanic ritual abuse scare of the 1980s‚Äîbeginning with the memoir
   [265]Michelle Remembers‚Äîwhich depicted Satanism as a vast
   [266]conspiracy of elites with a predilection for [267]child abuse and
   [268]human sacrifice.^[269][52]^[270][53] This genre frequently
   describes Satan as physically incarnating in order to receive
   worship.^[271][54]
